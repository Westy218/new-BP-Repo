Positioning Creates the Promise.
Working For You, or Against You?

Most don't give it the attention it deserves.

If your positioning is just a list of services rather than a specific commitment to the market, you are forcing yourself — and whoever you hand it to — to work backward.

Every professional practice runs on one sequence, whether you manage it or not:

  • 1
    Positioning creates the promise.A specific commitment to the market—not a service description, an explicit promise you make to your clients.
  • 2
    Marketing distributes it.Putting that promise in front of the right decision-makers, consistently. For most firms, that is what you say on your website or your LinkedIn profile.
  • 3
    Sales confirms it.Verifying, in the room, that the firm's capability matches the client's reality. That is what gets communicated by whoever answers the phone, in discovery meetings, and across your correspondence.
  • 4
    Delivery keeps it.The actual advisory and technical execution, measured against exactly what was promised. Yet most never bother to measure the result against Stage One.

Leave positioning undefined, and by default, you offer the exact same as most others in the market. Yet, you still expect the client you actually want to show up.

When they don't, you aren't just left frustrated—you end up settling for whoever fills the gap.

That is not a market problem—it is a broken promise.

You don't need more marketing tactics or a new sales script. You need the actual words to clearly define your promise to the clients you actually want to serve.

Why I Do This Work

Twenty-five years. The same wall.

I've spent over two decades working with professional service firms — firms with genuine expertise, real results, and unquestionable credibility in their field.

And I kept observing the same pattern.

Firm owners, year after year, delivering excellent work — yet stuck in the same feast-and-famine cycle, losing work to less experienced competitors, watching fees negotiated down by clients who didn't fully grasp what they were paying for.

I call these the photocopied years — the same effort, the same outcomes, no matter how good the work actually was.

The frustrating part? These weren't average firms. They were among the best in their field. And they knew it.

When I asked what was holding them back, the answers always pointed outward — the market had changed, referrals had dried up, clients didn't value expertise the way they used to. Everything except the actual cause.

That's when I saw the real pattern. I'd been handing out tactics — better proposals, more content, sharper outreach. Useful tools. But tactics applied to a broken Stage One promise produce the same result every time: more effort, the same ceiling.

The problem was never ability, or even effort. It was that Stage One — positioning — had never been explicitly locked. Marketing was distributing a promise nobody had defined. Sales was fighting to prove value that was never precisely stated. Delivery was absorbing clients who were never the right fit to begin with.

So I stopped handing out tactics, and started fixing Stage One first.

That's what the Reposition Session does. One hour, at Stage One, so everything downstream finally has something solid to work with.
